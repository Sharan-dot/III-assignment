About Me

Hi, I'm Sharan Muhhuku, a 22-year-old UX Design student from Uganda, currently in my second year at Wilfrid Laurier University, pursuing a Bachelor of Arts with a major in UX Design. I'm dedicated to crafting intuitive and user-centered digital experiences. With a keen eye for detail and a love for problem-solving, I strive to understand the needs of users and translate them into innovative design solutions.

Summary of the Maker Projects (GMP1 & GMP2)

In Maker Lab I, my group and I co-created two innovative projects: a shopping box design and an educational waste disposal game. The shopping box was designed to enhance convenience, featuring wheels, a handle, and grocery partitions for better usability. The educational game aimed to teach proper waste disposal through interactive gameplay, fostering environmental awareness. Both projects were user-centered, blending functionality and engagement to address real-world problems. These experiences allowed us to explore design thinking, prototyping, and collaboration in a hands-on environment.

Description of the Design Problem(s)

The shopping box project addressed the challenge of improving grocery shopping experiences, particularly for individuals who struggle with managing multiple bags or spills. We focused on creating a portable, organized, and durable solution. The waste disposal game tackled the issue of environmental awareness and improper waste management. The goal was to make learning about waste segregation engaging for users, particularly younger audiences, by turning it into an interactive and educational experience. Both projects sought to solve problems that resonate with daily life and societal challenges.

Review of Each Step in the Design Process

The design process began with problem identification, where we conducted research to understand pain points for shoppers and environmental education gaps. Empathize: User interviews and surveys informed us about grocery shopping struggles and knowledge gaps in waste disposal. Define: We defined clear user personas and needs: busy parents and environmentally-conscious educators. Ideate: Brainstorming sessions generated ideas like a compact, wheeled box with partitions and a game involving drag-and-drop waste categorization. Prototype: For GMP1, we built a cardboard model of the shopping box to test size and functionality. For GMP2, we developed a digital game prototype with interactive elements. Concluding Evaluation of the Final Designs; Both projects achieved their objectives with unique, functional designs. The shopping box was praised for its portability and organization features, though material durability could be improved. The educational game successfully engaged users but could benefit from additional levels to expand its reach. Overall, these projects taught us the importance of iterative design, adaptability, and user feedback. They marked significant steps in my growth as a UX designer, emphasizing problem-solving and teamwork.
